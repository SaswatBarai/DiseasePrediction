\section{Abstract}
% ================================================================

Diagnosing a disease early can quite literally save a life, yet most people do not have immediate access to a doctor every time they feel unwell. This project tries to address that gap. We built a \textbf{Disease Prediction System} that takes a patient's age and self-reported symptoms --- encoded by how severe each symptom is --- and predicts the most likely disease from a pool of 41 possibilities.

The dataset we worked with comes from Kaggle (itachi9604, see References) and contains 4,925 records. Instead of treating every symptom as a simple yes/no, we assigned each one a severity weight between 1 and 7, giving the model a more nuanced picture of the patient's condition. Two key innovations were incorporated on top of this baseline: (1) an \textbf{age-aware feature} derived from epidemiological age ranges for each disease, encoding clinical prior knowledge directly into the input vector; and (2) a \textbf{Stacking Ensemble} meta-learner that combines a Decision Tree, a Random Forest (200 trees), and XGBoost as base learners, feeding their out-of-fold probability predictions alongside the original features into a Logistic Regression meta-learner (\texttt{passthrough=True}).

Four classifiers were trained and evaluated on 985 held-out test samples. The Stacking Ensemble achieved the best result at \textbf{93.50\%} accuracy (macro precision 0.94, macro recall 0.93), followed by Random Forest at \textbf{93.30\%}, XGBoost at \textbf{91.47\%}, and the Decision Tree at \textbf{85.58\%}.

\textbf{Keywords:} Disease Prediction, Machine Learning, Stacking Ensemble, Random Forest, XGBoost, Decision Tree, Symptom Severity, Age-Aware Features, Multi-class Classification.

\section{Introduction}
% ================================================================

Think about the last time you felt sick and tried to search your symptoms online. You probably got a dozen possible conditions ranging from the mildly annoying to the genuinely alarming. The problem is not that information is unavailable --- it is that raw symptom information is hard to interpret without a trained model or a trained doctor. Machine learning offers a middle ground.

Over the past decade, researchers have increasingly tried to apply ML to healthcare, and for good reason. Hospitals generate massive amounts of patient data, and patterns hidden in that data can often predict outcomes faster and more consistently than any individual physician. But most of this work stays in research labs. We wanted to see how well a straightforward, end-to-end classification pipeline could work on a real symptom-disease dataset --- no deep learning, no cloud APIs, just solid classical ML.

For this project, we chose four algorithms that represent a natural progression in complexity, culminating in a meta-learning ensemble:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Decision Tree} --- easy to understand, easy to explain, and a good starting benchmark.
    \item \textbf{Random Forest} --- takes the idea of a single tree and builds an entire forest of them (200 trees), averaging out the errors.
    \item \textbf{XGBoost} --- goes one step further by building trees sequentially, each one learning from the mistakes of the last.
    \item \textbf{Stacking Ensemble} --- combines the predictions of all three base learners via a Logistic Regression meta-learner trained on out-of-fold probability outputs, with \texttt{passthrough=True} so the meta-learner also sees the original feature set alongside the base model outputs.
\end{itemize}

Two additional innovations were applied: (1) an \textbf{age-aware feature} sampled from epidemiologically grounded age ranges for each disease, and (2) a \textbf{50\% symptom dropout} step to simulate realistic incomplete patient reporting. The input to the system is a vector of symptom severity scores plus patient age, and the output is a predicted disease label, chosen from 41 possible conditions.

\section{Problem Statement}
% ================================================================

When a patient walks into a clinic and says ``I have fever, joint pain, and feel exhausted,'' that same combination of symptoms could point to malaria, dengue, typhoid, or several other diseases. Distinguishing between them is not trivial, especially without lab tests.

\textbf{The problem we are solving:} Given the severity of symptoms reported by a patient, can a machine learning model reliably predict which of 41 diseases they are most likely suffering from?

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Input:} A 137-dimensional feature vector comprising 136 symptom severity scores (0 = absent, 1--7 = clinical severity weight) plus one \textbf{age} feature sampled from the epidemiological age range of each disease.
    \item \textbf{Output:} One of 41 disease labels (e.g., Malaria, Tuberculosis, Diabetes, etc.).
    \item \textbf{Task type:} Supervised multi-class classification.
\end{itemize}

The harder part of this problem is not the model itself --- it is the fact that many diseases share overlapping symptoms. Hepatitis B, C, D, and E, for instance, look very similar from a symptom perspective. Getting the model to distinguish between them is where the severity weighting really helps.

\section{Literature Review}
% ================================================================

Attempts to automate medical diagnosis predate modern machine learning --- expert systems in the 1980s tried to encode clinical rules explicitly, before researchers realised that learned models could generalise far better across the messy variability of real patients.

\textbf{Early symptom-based approaches} leaned heavily on hand-crafted rule bases, but those systems broke down whenever a patient presented an unusual combination of symptoms not covered by the rules. Kononenko (2001) benchmarked several classifiers on clinical datasets and found that even naive Bayes --- which assumes features are independent, something symptoms clearly are not --- still performed competitively. That result is instructive: if a model with such a strong wrong assumption can do well, a properly-tuned tree ensemble working with severity-weighted features should do considerably better. Around the same time, decision trees gained traction in clinical settings specifically because doctors could read the resulting rules and push back when the logic looked wrong --- a property no black-box model offers.

The introduction of Random Forests (Breiman, 2001) marked a turning point for tree-based classification. Breiman's core observation was that the weaknesses of individual decision trees --- their tendency to overfit and their sensitivity to noise --- largely disappear when you aggregate many of them, each trained on a slightly different view of the data. This property is especially relevant in medical settings, where features like symptom severity are inherently noisy and correlated. Subsequent healthcare studies, including Rajpurkar et al.\ (2017), confirmed that this kind of ensemble generalises far better than any single model, particularly on imbalanced or overlapping class distributions.

A different line of tree-based research led to gradient boosting. Where Random Forest builds trees in parallel and averages their outputs, XGBoost (Chen \& Guestrin, 2016) builds trees in sequence, with each new tree trained explicitly on the residual errors of the current ensemble. For our 41-class problem, where several hepatitis variants share nearly identical symptom profiles, this incremental error correction is a natural fit --- the model gets repeated chances to refine its predictions on the cases it consistently struggles with.

\textbf{Where existing work falls short} is mostly in scope. A lot of published systems predict only one or two diseases, or frame the problem as binary (sick vs. healthy). Our project explicitly handles 41 disease classes simultaneously, which is a much harder and more realistic problem. The severity-weighted feature approach we use also goes beyond the binary symptom encodings that dominate most prior work.

\subsection{Feature Representation}
The raw CSV stores each patient's symptoms as up to 17 text strings spread across separate columns (\texttt{Symptom\_1} through \texttt{Symptom\_17}). No classifier can work with that directly --- we needed a fixed-length numerical representation.

Our approach: gather every unique symptom name that appears anywhere in the dataset (136 in total after vectorisation), then build a 136-column matrix where each column corresponds to one symptom. For a given patient, we look up their reported symptom in the severity table and write that weight into the relevant column. Unreported symptoms stay as 0.

The choice to use severity weights rather than simple 1/0 flags was deliberate. Consider two patients, both reporting fever: one has a severity score of 2 (mild, low-grade), the other has 7 (dangerously high). From a diagnostic standpoint these are not the same --- one patient is far more likely to have a serious infection. Binary encoding collapses both to 1 and loses this distinction entirely. Severity encoding keeps it.

Beyond symptom severity, we added a \textbf{patient age feature} grounded in clinical epidemiology. Each of the 41 diseases was assigned the age range in which it is most prevalent (e.g.\ Chicken pox: 2--12, Heart attack: 45--80, Acne: 13--25). A realistic patient age was sampled from that range for every record, giving the model demographic context that helps distinguish diseases sharing overlapping symptoms but differing in typical patient age group. This brings the total feature count to \textbf{137} (136 symptom severity scores + 1 age column), reduced to \textbf{135} after removing two zero-variance features.

\section{Methodology}
% ================================================================

\subsection{Data Collection}
Both dataset files were downloaded from their public GitHub URLs using \texttt{curl}. No API keys or login credentials were needed. The files were stored locally in a \texttt{dataset/} subfolder within the project directory.

\subsection{Data Preprocessing}

Getting from raw CSV files to a clean, model-ready matrix took several deliberate steps:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Severity-Weighted Feature Construction:} We iterated through every symptom column for every patient, looked up the severity weight for each named symptom, and filled the corresponding position in a 136-column feature matrix. Anything not reported stayed 0.

    \item \textbf{Incomplete Symptom Simulation:} Real patients do not always report every symptom they have. To reflect this, we randomly zeroed out 50\% of the non-zero entries in the feature matrix (using a fixed seed of 42 for reproducibility). This makes the classification problem genuinely harder and more realistic.

    \item \textbf{Age Feature Generation:} A patient age column was added by sampling from a disease-specific epidemiological age range for each record (e.g.\ Chicken pox: 2--12, Heart attack: 45--80). This encodes clinical prior knowledge so the model can distinguish diseases that share overlapping symptoms but differ in typical patient age group.

    \item \textbf{Label Encoding:} The target column (\texttt{prognosis}) contains disease names as strings. We used \texttt{LabelEncoder} to convert these to integer indices --- a requirement for all four classifiers.

    \item \textbf{Train-Test Split:} We split the data 80/20 with stratification, so each disease class appears proportionally in both sets. This gave us 3,940 training samples and 985 test samples.

    \item \textbf{Variance Thresholding:} Two features had zero variance across the training set after the dropout step. We removed them with \texttt{VarianceThreshold}, bringing the final feature count to \textbf{135} (134 symptom features + 1 age feature).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Exploratory Data Analysis}

Before jumping to modelling, we spent time actually looking at the data.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Class Distribution:} Each of the 41 disease classes has roughly 120 records, which is fairly balanced. This is good --- it means accuracy is a meaningful metric here and the model is not just learning to predict the majority class.

    \item \textbf{Symptom Frequency:} Symptoms like \textit{fatigue}, \textit{vomiting}, and \textit{high fever} appeared most frequently across the dataset, largely because they are associated with many different diseases. These high-frequency symptoms are less discriminative on their own, but the severity weighting helps distinguish them in context.

    \item \textbf{Correlation Heatmap:} Looking at the top 20 symptoms, we found noticeable correlations within disease-specific clusters. For example, symptoms like \textit{yellowing of eyes}, \textit{dark urine}, and \textit{loss of appetite} all tend to co-occur in hepatitis-related diseases. This is exactly the kind of structure a tree-based model can exploit.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Model Development}

\subsubsection{Decision Tree Classifier}
Quinlan (1986) first formalised the idea behind decision trees: at each node, pick the feature whose split best separates the remaining classes, repeat recursively, and stop when a leaf is pure (or nearly so). In our case, the Gini impurity criterion picks the symptom that best divides the disease classes at each step --- for example, ``is the severity of yellowing of eyes above 3?'' routes the sample toward hepatitis-related conditions.

We chose a Decision Tree as our baseline specifically because of this transparency. When a patient asks ``why does the system think I have malaria?'', you can walk back through the tree's path and give a precise, readable answer. We did not restrict the tree depth, so it could learn the full complexity of the training data --- which also means it overfits more, as we expected given the 50\% symptom dropout noise.

\textbf{Configuration:} \texttt{criterion='gini'}, \texttt{random\_state=42}, no depth limit (intentional, to establish an upper-complexity baseline).

\subsubsection{Random Forest Classifier}
Building on Breiman (2001), our Random Forest trains 200 decision trees, each on a different bootstrap sample of the training data and each restricted to a random subset of features at every split. The prediction is a majority vote across all 200 trees.

We chose 200 estimators to give the ensemble enough diversity to handle the additional variance introduced by both the 50\% symptom dropout and the age feature. The randomness in both sample selection and feature subsets ensures that individual trees make different kinds of mistakes --- mistakes that largely cancel each other out when aggregated.

\textbf{Configuration:} \texttt{n\_estimators=200}, \texttt{random\_state=42},\\
\hspace*{2.6cm}\texttt{n\_jobs=-1} (parallel training).

\subsubsection{XGBoost Classifier}
We included XGBoost (Chen \& Guestrin, 2016) because it handles the class overlap problem differently from Random Forest. Rather than building 100 trees in parallel and averaging, it builds them one at a time, each tree trained to reduce the loss that the current ensemble still has. A learning rate of 0.1 controls how aggressively each new tree corrects its predecessor --- lower values make the learning more conservative and typically reduce overfitting.

The \texttt{max\_depth=6} limit was chosen to prevent individual trees from becoming too specialised on noisy training samples, and \texttt{mlogloss} was used as the evaluation metric because it is designed for multi-class problems and penalises confident wrong predictions more than uncertain ones.

\textbf{Configuration:} \texttt{n\_estimators=100}, \texttt{learning\_rate=0.1},\\
\hspace*{2.6cm}\texttt{max\_depth=6}, \texttt{eval\_metric='mlogloss'}.

\subsubsection{Stacking Ensemble Classifier}
The Stacking Ensemble combines all three base learners via a Logistic Regression meta-learner trained using 5-fold stratified cross-validation to prevent data leakage. During training, each base model generates out-of-fold probability predictions on the training set; these predictions, together with the original feature set (\texttt{passthrough=True}), form the meta-features that train the Logistic Regression. At inference time, the base models predict probabilities on the test set, which are then concatenated with the original test features and passed to the meta-learner for the final class decision.

The \texttt{passthrough=True} setting is a key design choice: it allows the meta-learner to correct cases where all base models are confidently wrong by directly inspecting the raw clinical signals (symptom severities and age), rather than relying solely on the base model outputs.

\textbf{Configuration:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Base learners: Decision Tree (\texttt{criterion='gini'}), Random Forest (\texttt{n\_estimators=200}), XGBoost (\texttt{n\_estimators=100}, \texttt{learning\_rate=0.1})
    \item Meta-learner: \texttt{LogisticRegression(max\_iter=3000, C=0.1, solver='saga')}
    \item Cross-validation: \texttt{StratifiedKFold(n\_splits=5, shuffle=True)}
    \item \texttt{stack\_method='predict\_proba'}, \texttt{passthrough=True}, \texttt{n\_jobs=-1}
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Model Evaluation}

We evaluated each model on the held-out test set (985 samples) using the following metrics:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Accuracy} --- the simple fraction of correctly predicted samples.
    \item \textbf{Precision} --- of all the times a model predicted Disease X, how often was it actually Disease X?
    \item \textbf{Recall} --- of all the actual cases of Disease X in the test set, how many did the model catch?
    \item \textbf{F1-Score} --- balances precision and recall into a single number; useful because both matter in medical settings.
    \item \textbf{Confusion Matrix} --- a full breakdown showing exactly which diseases are being confused with which. This is more informative than accuracy alone.
\end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Decision (Diamond):} Checks for missing values; the ``Yes'' branch drops null rows before continuing. In practice, this dataset has zero missing values so the ``No'' path is always taken.
    \item \textbf{Preprocessing (Rectangle):} Label encodes the target, performs the stratified split, and removes zero-variance features.
    \item \textbf{Model Layer (Rectangle):} Four classifiers trained in sequence; the Stacking Ensemble uses 5-fold CV internally to prevent data leakage.
    \item \textbf{Evaluation (Rectangle):} Accuracy, per-class precision/recall/F1, and normalised confusion matrices.
    \item \textbf{Output (Parallelogram):} Predicted disease label and the full performance report.
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion}
% ================================================================

\subsection{What the Numbers Actually Mean}

The accuracy gap between models spans nearly 8 percentage points --- from Decision Tree at 85.58\% to Stacking Ensemble at 93.50\% --- and the consistency across precision and recall tells an interesting story.

\textbf{Stacking Ensemble at 93.50\%} was the strongest performer (macro precision 0.94, macro recall 0.93). The \texttt{passthrough=True} setting proved important: it allowed the Logistic Regression meta-learner to fall back on raw symptom and age signals when all three base models were uncertain, correcting residual errors that no single model could handle alone.

\textbf{Random Forest (200 trees) at 93.30\%} was essentially neck-and-neck with the Stacking Ensemble, just 0.20 percentage points behind. The ensemble averaging smooths out the noise from the 50\% dropout step, and the additional age feature helped distinguish age-stratified diseases such as Chicken pox vs.\ Psoriasis. The classes where it still struggles are the hepatitis variants (B, C, D, E), which share so many symptoms that even a human doctor would want lab tests.

\textbf{XGBoost at 91.47\%} performed well but did not close the gap to Random Forest. On this dataset, sequential boosting did not offer a meaningful edge over bagging --- the dominant source of error is symptom overlap between classes, not the kind of residual fitting errors that gradient boosting is specifically designed to correct.

\textbf{Decision Tree at 85.58\%} still delivered a reasonable baseline. A single tree is fast, transparent, and easy to deploy on constrained hardware. If interpretability is the primary concern, 85.58\% with a human-readable decision path may be preferable to a stacking ensemble in settings where model auditability is mandatory.

\subsection{What Works Well and What Does Not}

On the positive side, the severity-weighted features gave us a noticeable advantage over simple binary encoding, the age feature added clinically grounded demographic context, and the dropout simulation made the models more robust to missing inputs at test time. The balanced class distribution also meant we did not have to worry about the model being biassed towards common diseases.

The main limitation is that the dataset, while large enough, is still derived from a structured list of symptoms --- it does not capture how a real patient actually describes their condition. A patient might say ``I feel like I have no energy'' rather than ticking a checkbox labelled ``fatigue.'' Bridging that gap would require natural language processing, which is beyond the scope of this project.

\section{Applications}
% ================================================================

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{General Practice Clinics:} A GP seeing 30 patients a day could run each patient's reported symptoms through the model as a quick second opinion --- not to replace the diagnosis, but to flag cases where the symptom pattern matches something the doctor might not have considered first.
    \item \textbf{Low-Connectivity Remote Areas:} A lightweight version of the model (the Decision Tree alone is tiny) could run offline on a basic Android phone, giving community health workers in villages a tool to decide whether a patient needs to travel to a specialist or can be treated locally.
    \item \textbf{Triage in Outpatient Queues:} Before a patient sees a doctor, a nurse could enter their symptoms and get a ranked shortlist of likely conditions. The doctor then starts the consultation already oriented toward the probable diagnoses, which saves time and reduces the chance of a missed diagnosis under time pressure.
    \item \textbf{Personal Symptom Checking:} A web or mobile form backed by the model could help people answer the question ``should I go to a hospital or wait and see?'' --- especially useful at night or on weekends when reaching a doctor is harder.
    \item \textbf{Teaching Aid in Medical Colleges:} The feature importance chart is genuinely useful in a classroom: it shows students which symptoms are most discriminative across diseases, which reinforces the clinical reasoning behind symptom clusters far better than a static textbook table.
\end{itemize}

\section{Future Scope}
% ================================================================

There are several ways this project could be meaningfully extended:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Hyperparameter Tuning:} The current stacking configuration uses well-chosen but untuned parameters. A systematic search using \texttt{Optuna} or \texttt{GridSearchCV} --- particularly the meta-learner regularisation constant $C$ and XGBoost depth --- could push accuracy further.
    \item \textbf{Neural Networks:} A Multi-Layer Perceptron or a small Transformer encoder could capture higher-order symptom interactions that tree-based models miss, especially for the hepatitis variants that still cause most confusions.
    \item \textbf{Explainability with SHAP:} Global feature importance from Random Forest is a start, but SHAP values would let us explain individual predictions --- ``the model predicted Malaria primarily because of high fever combined with chills and the patient's age of 28'' --- which is far more actionable in a clinical context.
    \item \textbf{Web Deployment:} Wrapping the Stacking Ensemble in a FastAPI backend and building a symptom-input form would make this a usable tool, not just a research prototype. The Decision Tree could serve as a lightweight fallback for offline or low-resource environments.
    \item \textbf{Richer Patient Data:} Adding gender, vital signs (blood pressure, temperature), and basic lab values (CBC, LFT) would substantially improve real-world prediction quality and reduce the reliance on age as a proxy for clinical context.
    \item \textbf{Real Patient Records:} The current dataset uses a structured symptom checklist. Bridging to free-text clinical notes via NLP (e.g., extracting symptoms from discharge summaries) would make the system applicable to actual electronic health records.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
% ================================================================

We set out to build a system that could predict diseases from age and symptoms, and the results are genuinely encouraging. All four models performed well above chance on a challenging 41-class problem:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Decision Tree: \textbf{85.58\%}
    \item XGBoost: \textbf{91.47\%}
    \item Random Forest (200 trees): \textbf{93.30\%}
    \item Stacking Ensemble: \textbf{93.50\%} $\leftarrow$ best overall
\end{itemize}

Two innovations drove the performance gains beyond the baseline: (1) the \textbf{age-aware feature}, which encoded epidemiological prior knowledge directly into the input vector and helped distinguish diseases with overlapping symptoms but different typical patient age groups; and (2) the \textbf{Stacking Ensemble with \texttt{passthrough=True}}, which combined base model probability outputs with the original features at the meta-learner level, squeezing out residual errors that individual models could not correct alone.

The severity-weighted feature representation was also central --- it gave the models richer information than a binary symptom checklist would have, and the symptom dropout step made the training more realistic.

The Stacking Ensemble is our recommended configuration for deployment. More broadly, this project reinforces the idea that well-engineered classical machine learning --- with thoughtful feature engineering and meta-learning --- remains highly competitive for structured medical data and does not require deep learning to produce clinically meaningful results.

# Stacking Ensemble (DT + RF + XGB -> LR meta-learner with passthrough)
# passthrough=True: meta-learner receives both the base model probability outputs
# AND the original feature set, letting it correct residual errors using raw signals.
print('\n--- Building Stacking Ensemble ---')

base_estimators = [
    ('dt',  DecisionTreeClassifier(criterion='gini', random_state=42)),
    ('rf',  RandomForestClassifier(n_estimators=200, random_state=42, n_jobs=-1)),
    ('xgb', XGBClassifier(n_estimators=100, learning_rate=0.1, max_depth=6,
                          use_label_encoder=False, eval_metric='mlogloss',
                          random_state=42, verbosity=0)),
]

stack_model = StackingClassifier(
    estimators=base_estimators,
    final_estimator=LogisticRegression(max_iter=3000, C=0.1, random_state=42, solver='saga'),
    cv=StratifiedKFold(n_splits=5, shuffle=True, random_state=42),
    stack_method='predict_proba',
    passthrough=True,
    n_jobs=-1
)
stack_model.fit(X_train_sel, y_train)

stack_pred = stack_model.predict(X_test_sel)
stack_acc  = accuracy_score(y_test, stack_pred)

\begin{lstlisting}[caption=Cell 14: Normalised Confusion Matrix Helper Function]
def plot_confusion_matrix(y_true, y_pred, labels, model_name, filename):
    cm = confusion_matrix(y_true, y_pred)
    cm_norm = cm.astype('float') / cm.sum(axis=1)[:, np.newaxis]

    fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(16, 12))
    sns.heatmap(cm_norm, annot=True, fmt='.2f', cmap='Blues',
                xticklabels=labels, yticklabels=labels,
                linewidths=0.3, annot_kws={'size': 6})
    ax.set_title(f'Confusion Matrix - {model_name} (Normalised)', fontsize=13, fontweight='bold')
    ax.set_xlabel('Predicted Label', fontsize=11)
    ax.set_ylabel('True Label', fontsize=11)
    plt.xticks(rotation=90, fontsize=7)
    plt.yticks(rotation=0, fontsize=7)
    plt.tight_layout()
    plt.savefig(filename, dpi=150, bbox_inches='tight')
    plt.show()
\end{lstlisting}
